New Study Finds Strong Correlation Between Academic Achievement, Not Falling Asleep in Class

A new study published by a research group at Columbia University has unearthed a startling trend in academic performance. In addition to the more well-known predictors of academic success, such as socioeconomic status and family structure, the evidence gathered indicates that students who fall asleep in class also tend to receive lower grades on assignments and exams.
"What we're seeing here is very alarming," research head BobeMleby says. "Our data is showing us a remarkably strong correlation between high academic performance and remaining awake during lectures and tests. This is something that will be difficult for many to accept, but we hope that our findings will help convince schools and teachers to restructure their classes in order to meet these students' needs. After all, we don't want these sleeping students to fall behind in their coursework due to unfair biases. They simply have a different learning style than others, and it's the school's responsibility to accommodate their need to fall asleep every period."
"This is really very intriguing," Mleby continues. "No one really expected that staying awake in class would be so integral to academic success. But the data in this case is very clear."
This study came on the heels of previous research by the same team that found that academic achievement is also positively correlated with caring about the coursework and actually trying to learn something before the test.
